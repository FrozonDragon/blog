Coding

Coding is an intellectually challenging and exhausting activity.

Preparedness

  1. First, your code must work.
  2. Your code must solve the problem set for you by the customer.

    Often the customer’s requirements do not actually solve the customer’s problems. It is up to you to see this and negotiate with the customer to ensure that the customer’s true needs are met.

  3. Your code must fit well into the existing system.
  4. Your code must be readable by other programmers.

3 AM CODE

Don’t write code when you are tired.

WORRY CODE

  • Professional developers allocate their personal time in order to ensure that the time spent at the office is as productive as possible.
  • If you find yourself at the office and the background anxieties are sapping your productivity, then it is better to spend an hour quieting them than to use brute force to write code that you’ll just have to throw away later (or worse, live with).

The Flow Zone

The hyper-productive state, which is called “the Flow” or “the Zone”, is the highly focused, tunnel-vision state of consciousness that programmers can get into while they write code.

MUSIC

Personal Comment: Not everyone works effeciently while listening music. At least, music doesnot work for me to focus or cool down.

INTERRUPTIONS

  • Pairing can be very helpful as a way to deal with interruptions.
  • TDD (Test Driven Development) is another big help.

There will be interruptions that distract you and cause you to lose time. When they happen, remember that next time you may be the one who needs to interrupt someone else. So the professional attitude is a polite willingness to be helpful.

Personal Note: Any methods to deal with interruption is finding a way to store the context before interruption and switch back to the context after dealing with interruption. Men tends to think or behavior like CPUs?

Writer’s Block

You sit at your workstation and nothing happens.
The solution: Find a pair partner.

Personal Note: How to make sure you will not invade his/her flow zone?

CREATIVE INPUT

Creativity breeds creativity

Personal Note: To find some activities to recover your momentum. Such activites may only work for you!

Debugging

Debugging time is just as expensive to the business as coding time is, and therefore anything we can do to avoid or diminish it is good.
Whether you adopt TDD or some other discipline of equal efficacy, it is incumbent upon you as a professional to reduce your debugging time as close to zero as you can get.

Pacing Yourself

Software development is a marathon, not a sprint. You can’t win the race by trying to run as fast as you can from the outset. You win by conserving your resources and pacing yourself. A marathon runner takes care of her body both before and during the race. Professional programmers conserve their energy and creativity with the same care.

Being Late

Regularly measure your progress against your goal, and come up with three fact-based end dates: best case, nominal case, and worst case. Be as honest as you can about all three dates.
Do not incorporate hope into your estimates!
Present all three numbers to your team and stakeholders. Update these numbers daily.

Help

Programming is hard. The younger you are the less you believe this. After all, it’s just a bunch of if and while statements. But as you gain experience you begin to realize that the way you combine those if and while statements is critically important.

Personal Node: I cannot agree any more! Especially, the program you are maintaining is critical.

  • HELPING OTHERS

    It is a violation of professional ethics to sequester yourself in a cubicle or office and refuse the queries of others.

  • BEING HELPED

    Do not protect your turf!
    The reality is that programmers do not tend to be collaborators, hence we require disciplines that drive us to collaborate.

  • MENTORING

    It is a matter of professional ethics for senior programmers to spend time taking younger programmers under their wing and mentoring them.
